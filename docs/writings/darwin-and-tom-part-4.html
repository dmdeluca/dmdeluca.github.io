Darwin and Tom

“What should I write about?” Tom wondered. His life never seemed remarkable to him, in all honesty, but ever since Darwin mentioned this book, he felt a certain urgency to leave something behind. But who would read such a book? A book about a man who — what was it he did? — wandered around in the woods with his friend? It had been done before: Bill Bryson, A Walk in the Woods. He remembered from high school. And how many narrated forest treks could a reader really stomach? He swallowed his spit between cold breaths in time with the metered crunching of snow beneath his snowshoes. If I wrote a book and people wanted to read it, he reasoned, it couldn’t possibly be about me. It would have to be about someone else, someone more interesting.

In his reflection, Tom let out a sigh that was perhaps more audible than he intended, and Darwin, who strode about ten meters ahead, turned to face his follower. Sweaty black curls peeked out from under his hat and stopped just above flat, piercing blue eyes. The winter air made clouds out of his breath.

“Are you doing all right, there?” he asked, feigning genuine concern with machinelike precision.

Panting from the ascent, Tom replied in spurts. “I was just, uh — I was thinking — about the book we were — we were talking about.”

“The book?” Darwin spoke casually, pretending not to remember.

There was a silence. “The book. You know, the trail? I’m supposed to leave a trail? We were talking about it all last night.”

“Oh, that.” Darwin smiled: Tom had been thinking about it all morning. “Don’t worry about that, Tom. I was just playing around.” Darwin baited him. “I didn’t mean to upset you.”

“I’m not upset about it, I just — I was thinking about what kind of story I could even tell. I don’t think I live an interesting enough life to write about it, you know?”

Darwin looked at Tom for a moment, proud of the silent avalanche he had set in motion, and then away. With the eyes of a predator, he followed the trunk of a nearby spruce to check to source of a sparkle of birdsong. The sun shone a bright yellow through frosted boughs. The stillness of all this is good for transformation, Darwin thought. The emptiness, freedom from distraction. Where else but here in the mountains, in the callous heart of winter, could a person be subjected so mercilessly to their own demons? Where else could a person be forced to evolve? He stopped himself, conscious of the temptation to bend to mystique, but reveled in what he saw as a successful experiment. He returned his gaze to Tom, drawn to this moment of vulnerability like a vulture.

“You don’t think you’re interesting?” he asked. “What makes you say that? You’re out here with me, aren’t you?”

“Well, yes, but it’s been done,” Tom insisted. “This trail, these mountains. It’s not like I’m climbing Everest, Dar’. It’s small stuff compared to, you know, all the other crazy things people out there do.”

“It’s not small stuff to you, though, is it? It’s more than you’ve ever done.”

Tom took the comment at face-value, ignoring the implication. “Well, yes, but that’s not the point.”

“What’s the point, Tom?” Darwin sharpened his tone, letting out a shadow of a smile. “Why are you not worth writing about?”

Not worth writing about. Tom felt a floor fall away in the pit of his stomach and a darkness start to creep into the sunlit forest around him. The moss and the snow and the glistening drops of water hanging from the bottoms of the laden spruce branches seemed to pale. The question stung him, almost as if it were designed to do so. But that’s not Darwin’s fault, thought Tom. Just because I’m hurt doesn’t mean he meant to hurt me. I’m just sensitive. Yes, I’m just a sensitive person.

“Tom?” Darwin prodded, watching the process of dissolution.

“Can we talk about something else? Like, can we stop talking about this book?” Tom was exasperated. “It just makes me feel like I’m this — this boring, weak person with nothing to say — all because I can’t think of what to write a book about.” Wide-eyed, Tom gesticulated with his trekking poles. “Why do we all have to write books? Some people write books, some people don’t. Some people sing. It’s not for me, maybe. Maybe I’m just the guy who follows the trail. Maybe I’m just the guy who read the books that other people write, because, you know, they need an audience, don’t they? They need readers, right?” And even if I did try to write a book, it would be a waste of my time because I could have been out here all day enjoying the real trails and the real trees and the real fucking air, but no — I wanted to create a false image of it for people to look at instead, because I thought I was such a talented fucking storyteller that I could wrap it all up into a neat little package for some person to pick up and thumb through and say, Hmmm, that’s such a powerful take on the White Mountains! I never thought of it that way. Blasphemy! Fucking blasphemy.” He swung at a nearby tree with his pole, connected, and was startled by the loud crack. “So rather than fuck up the rest of this hike dreaming of the most boring and pointless book anyone has ever read, I think I’ll just keep hiking through the real world and stay out of my head. And who gives a fuck if I’m 'the tree that falls in forest'? I fell! It’s my business! Who needs to know?”

Darwin took off his hat, brushed it against his knee to get the snow off it, and put it back on. It was exhausting to be with Tom sometimes. It really was. The negativity welled from a source so deep Darwin thought it might never actually heal. The raw agitation with which he deliberated about his own worth, his own soul, disturbed and intrigued Darwin like the burning of a car wreck. So much smoke for such a small fire, too, he thought. But through the smoke Darwin saw the twisted steel frame of Tom’s spirit, the raw materials, the scrap. If I could just burn him to the point where the metal is hot enough to bend, then I could create something truly beautiful.

Darwin stepped down the steep blanket of snow with calculating mastery, the teeth of his snowshoes biting into it hungrily. He met Tom’s reddened eyes, which still smoldered with anger and self-hatred.

“The book was just an idea, Tom,” Darwin spoke with a precise gentleness. “There’s more to life than — ”

“It’s not about the book, you asshole,” Thomas snapped. “It’s about me, what I am. I feel like you want me to be something I’m not, but I just can’t be. I’m this person. I’m this way.”

“I don’t want to change you, Tom,” Darwin lied. “I’m your friend, we’re just out here on the trail hiking. We’re having a good time. We don’t have to set any records, and you don’t have to write a book. It sounds like the conversation we had last night left you feeling out of sorts, and that’s OK.” He put a gloved hand on Tom’s shoulder. “It’s OK to feel like this.”

“What are you, some kind of therapist? Get your hand off me!”

Too obvious, thought Darwin. Subtle, subtle. Apologize.

“I’m sorry,” Offer nothing more than that. Simple. Keep it open-ended. His mind will fill in the blanks. “I’m sorry, Tom.”

Tom took in heavy breaths and exhaled great clouds of steam. Darwin’s eyes appeared like glass, his eyebrows relaxed, his face like a plastic mask. There was no comfort in those features, those alien features. Emotion beaded and flowed off of Darwin’s soul like rain on a tarp, never passing through, never sinking in. He was like a one-way mirror, impervious and omniscient.

“It’s fine, Darwin.” Tom said, his gaze fixed on a nearby spruce. He could not look into Darwin’s glass eyes. “Let’s just — let’s just keep going. I’ll feel better. Let’s just keep going.” He added a lie. “I probably just need some food.”

Darwin nodded. “Right, food. Probably just hangry.” A reassuring smile slackened as he turned away.

The dry, metered crunching of Tom and Darwin’s snowshoes resumed, relieving the singular dead silence of winter; but a darkness lingered around Tom like a cloud. For the first time since starting this journey, Tom felt afraid. He felt afraid and unquestionably alone.
